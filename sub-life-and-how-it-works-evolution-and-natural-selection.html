Evolution and Natural Selection

Life on Earth varies in size, shape, colour and function. But if all life started from a common ancestor like a simple cell, how did everything become so different?

From a single cell to a dinosaur or a bird or an elephant, life underwent a process called evolution. Evolution is a complex, random process of trial and error. When an organisms DNA, (the blueprint for how it is made) undergoes mutation and makes a new design, the new design may or might not work. If the new design works, the organism is likely to survive and be around for a lot longer. This process is known as natural selection.

These new designs use both the organism's environment and the others they interact with creating for example, near-invisible birds, trickster plants and frozen-alive frogs!

All life stems from a single ancestor and is a result of countless mutations... you could even say that were ALL mutants!

Natural Selection

Natural selection was introduced as part of the theory of evolution by Charles Darwin. Darwin was a natural historian, and first mentioned natural selection in his book 'On the Origin of Species', which was published in 1859.
