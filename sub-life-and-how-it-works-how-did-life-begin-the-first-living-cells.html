The First Living Cells

The first living cells, the 'prokaryotes', arose from the primordial soup of the Earth's early lakes and oceans around 3.5 billion years ago. They exploded in number and spread across the world with some still surviving today!

If you travel to Western Australia, you will find under the shallow water hundreds of small stacks that look like rocks. These 'rocks' are called 'stromatolites' and are millions of prokaryotes called cyanobacteria living all clumped together as one. To become a stromatolite, each cell joins with another and forms a slimy layer which traps sand and grit. As they continue to grow, a new layer forms on top of the old one and the process continues.

Stromatolites are an incredibly important part of our history and without them most of life today would not be here. Of the many groups of prokaryotes, they were the first to make oxygen gas!
